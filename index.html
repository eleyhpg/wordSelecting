Sintaxis
of Racism

Omissions and context between inaugural addresses and African American speeches.

"Words have no power to impress the mind without the exquisite horror of their reality." - Edgar Allan Poe.
Poe wasn’t really involved in the politics of his time, making the “words” he talks about probably more aligned with the prose and narration of stories. But, in the end, aren’t politics a central argument for the storytelling of history? Although interpretations may always be personal, Poe’s quote is here taken as a statement: Words do not mean a thing when they don’t have a context. There’s no neutrality in the way words work. There’s no neutrality, period. Behind any articulation of language lies an intention. The use of context, repetition, order and omission of key terms will be analysed here during three important moments of American Racial History: the American Civil War, the Civil Rights movement, and today’s accounts. The intention of this project is not an attempt to showcase a “big picture”, but rather portray the relationship between history and race in the words of the men in power (via Presidential Inaugural Addresses) and those who fought for racial equality (via racial liberation speeches). But isn’t it expected to see more words like “Negro” or “slavery” in Black History Speeches than in the Inaugural Addresses made by the presidents? Yes, and that’s why this visualization has been calibrated to make an average of total apparition of a keyword/total number of words in the text to make a “fair” comparison. But there are other questions trying to be addressed here. Are the omissions on a speech justified by their context? By the priorities of a Nation against the priorities of black people? Are words limited by an immediate context (the place of a speech), or can they turn into a more broad context, without the aid of a “historic” eye? A white historic eye for that matter. Disclaimer for all of us white/non-black with no credentials or expertise on racial matters and lack of first hand experience: The mention (and omission) of these key terms becomes a gateway to formulate time-centered questions about personal agendas and tensions between the two not quite opposite sides of racial history in the United States of America. There is an intention based on the cynicism with which we approach the decisions made by powerful people and Governments. We must always proceed with caution when our sight is biased, when we align ourselves with the “good side” thinking that we know enough about past mistakes that, if called upon to take part of a similar plight, we’d do the obvious right thing. By thinking we’re experts in race, justice and mortality, based only on our perception of a “globalized” and “multicultural” world, we only expose our own not-so-innocent incompetence. Let us be aware of this and approach the comparisons here made with the openness of an observer. This visualization operates between quantitative and qualitative data, making it less about exact statistics and more about analysis and connections. Because of this, it’s important to state that all texts being studied here are the original transcripts provided by Yale’s Law Archive and the Black Past Organization.
